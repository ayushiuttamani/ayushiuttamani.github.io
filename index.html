HW5 - UFO Sightings Visualization

For this assignment I used the NUFORC UFO sightings dataset which contains over 80,000 reported UFO sightings from around the world. The data includes information like the date and time of the sighting, the city, state, and country, the shape of the UFO, how long it lasted, and the latitude/longitude coordinates.

Plot 1: UFO Sightings by Shape (Interactive)

This bar chart shows the number of UFO sightings for the top 10 most commonly reported shapes. The x-axis uses a nominal encoding to represent each UFO shape category (like "light", "triangle", "circle", etc.), sorted by count so the most common shape is on the left. The y-axis uses a quantitative encoding for the count of sightings. I colored the bars by country using the Tableau10 color scheme, which gives enough distinct colors to tell the countries apart. I chose Tableau10 because it works well for categorical variables with a moderate number of categories, and it looks clean without being too flashy.

For data transformations, I dropped rows with missing country values and filled in missing shapes with "unknown". I cleaned up text fields by stripping whitespace and standardizing capitalization. I also filtered the data down to only the top 10 most common shapes so the chart wouldn't be too crowded, and I aggregated the counts by grouping on shape and country.

The interactivity here is a dropdown selector that lets you filter by country. This is really useful because the US dominates the overall counts so heavily that it's hard to see patterns for other countries. When you pick "GB" or "CA" for example, you can see which shapes are most commonly reported in those countries, making it way easier to compare across regions.

Plot 2: UFO Sightings Over Time by Shape

This line chart shows how the frequency of UFO sightings has changed from 1940 to 2014, broken down by the top 5 most commonly reported shapes. The x-axis uses an ordinal encoding for year with labels every 5 years so it doesn't get too cramped, and the y-axis uses a quantitative encoding for the count of sightings per year. Each line represents a different shape and I used the Dark2 color scheme from ColorBrewer. I chose Dark2 because it gives good contrast between a small number of lines on a white background, so you can actually follow each shape's trend without squinting.

For transformations, I filtered the data to only include years between 1940 and 2014 since there are very few sightings before 1940 and the data cuts off around 2014. I limited it to the top 5 shapes to keep the chart readable and grouped by year and shape to count the sightings per group. One interesting thing you can see is the huge spike starting in the mid-1990s, especially for shapes like "light" and "triangle" - probably related to the rise of the internet making it easier to report sightings.
